# A Consumer’s Guide to Auto Insurance

## About the NAIC

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) is the oldest association of state government officials. Its members consist of the chief insurance regulators in all 50 states, the District of Columbia, and five U.S. territories. The primary responsibility of the state regulators is to protect the interests of insurance consumers, and the NAIC helps regulators fulfill that obligation in a number of ways. This guide is one example of work done by the NAIC to assist states in educating and protecting consumers.

Another way the NAIC supports state regulators is by providing a forum for the development of uniform public policy when uniformity is appropriate. It does this through model laws, regulations, and guidelines developed for the states’ use. States may adopt these models as-is or modify them to meet their marketplace and consumer needs.

The NAIC’s mission is to assist state insurance regulators, individually and collectively, in serving the public interest and achieving the following fundamental insurance regulatory goals in a responsive, efficient, and cost-effective manner, consistent with the wishes of its members:

- Protect the public interest;
- Promote competitive markets;
- Facilitate the fair and equitable treatment of insurance consumers;
- Promote the reliability, solvency, and financial solidity of insurance institutions; and
- Support and improve state regulation of insurance.

## Why You Need Auto Insurance

Auto insurance is an important purchase for most drivers. There are three main reasons to buy auto insurance:

1. **To comply with state laws**  
Most states have auto insurance requirements. Driving without insurance could result in fines and vehicle impoundment.

2. **To satisfy your lender**  
If you have a car loan, most lenders require you to carry insurance to protect their interest. If your insurance lapses, your lender may insure the vehicle at a higher premium and less coverage until you obtain your own policy.

3. **To protect your assets**  
Auto insurance provides bodily injury and property damage liability coverage for accidents for which you are responsible. It also covers legal costs if you are sued. State minimum coverages may not fully protect your assets. Auto insurance may also cover repairs to your insured vehicle, medical bills, and lost wages resulting from an accident.

## Coverages in an Auto Policy

An auto insurance policy includes several types of protections—some required and some optional. Some coverages may automatically be included unless you opt out. You should choose coverages that best meet your needs, which may mean selecting more than the minimum required.

Your auto policy may include the following coverages, each priced separately:

### Required by State Law
- **Bodily Injury Liability:** Covers injuries you cause to others. You and family members listed on the policy are also covered when driving someone else’s car with permission.
- **Property Damage Liability:** Pays for damages caused to others’ cars or objects and structures hit by your vehicle.

### Required by Law in Some States
- **Uninsured and Underinsured Motorist Coverage:** Reimburses you if an uninsured or hit-and-run driver hits you. Underinsured motorist coverage pays when the at-fault driver’s insurance is insufficient.
- **Medical Payments or Personal Injury Protection (PIP):** Pays for injuries to you and passengers. PIP can also cover lost wages and funeral costs in no-fault states. In no-fault states, your own insurer pays regardless of fault, but you can still sue the at-fault driver for serious injuries. You must file claims with the at-fault driver’s insurer for vehicle damage.

### Required by Your Lender if You Have an Auto Loan
- **Collision:** Pays for damage from a collision with another vehicle, object, pothole, or rollover.
- **Comprehensive:** Reimburses for damage not caused by a collision, including theft, hail, windstorm, flood, fire, and hitting animals. It also covers windshield damage, sometimes without a deductible.

Most insurers also offer additional coverages such as towing and rental car coverage when your vehicle is disabled. Equipment not permanently installed (like stereos, navigation systems, or cell phones) is generally not covered. Auto insurance does not cover paying off your loan if your car’s market value is less than what you owe; GAP insurance can cover this gap.

If you drive into Canada, your auto insurance covers you. For Mexico, you need Mexican auto insurance. Additional vehicle types such as motorcycles, ATVs, off-road vehicles, RVs, and commercial vehicles require separate policies.

## How Insurers Determine Your Premium

Many factors influence your premium, including the insurance company you choose. Different insurers use various methods to rate risk and set premiums.

Factors affecting your premium include:

- **Driving record:** Past 3-5 years of your and others’ driving records.
- **Credit-based insurance score:** Better scores often mean lower premiums. Some states restrict or ban the use of credit scores.
- **Age, gender, and marital status:** Younger drivers, males, singles, and households with young drivers tend to pay more. Seniors over 65 may also face higher premiums.
- **Type of vehicle:** Newer, more expensive, sports, or high-performance vehicles cost more to insure. Larger SUVs and trucks may also increase liability costs.
- **Location:** Urban areas typically have more accidents and thefts.
- **Vehicle use:** Annual mileage affects risk.
- **Prior insurance coverage:** Lack of coverage or only minimum coverage can increase premiums.
- **Previous claims:** Claims reported to databases like CLUE influence your premium.
- **Liability limits and deductibles:** Higher coverage limits and lower deductibles usually increase premiums.

### Credit-Based Insurance Scoring
Most insurers use your credit report to calculate a credit-based insurance score, which correlates with claim likelihood. This score differs from your regular credit score.

### Discounts
Ask your insurer about discounts for:
- Bundling auto and home insurance
- Multiple vehicles
- Safety devices (airbags, anti-lock brakes, anti-theft)
- Defensive driving courses (especially if 55+)
- Memberships or employer affiliations

## Smart Shopping

Different insurers charge different rates for the same coverage, and service levels vary. Shopping around is advisable.

Insurance can be sold via:
- **Independent agents:** Represent multiple companies and provide several quotes.
- **Exclusive agents:** Represent only one insurer.
- **Direct sales:** Online, mail, or phone.

Insurers typically pay agents via commissions or bonuses. To find reputable insurers and agents, ask friends, browse online, check the phone book, or look at advertisements. Your state insurance department may publish premium comparison guides.

Customer service quality can be gauged through complaint indexes, which many state departments publish. Verify licensing status with your state insurance department to avoid unlicensed agents or companies, which may not pay claims or refund premiums.

Check the financial strength of insurers through independent ratings agencies like Standard & Poor’s, A.M. Best, or Moody’s.

## Your Responsibilities

### Getting Premium Quotes
Compare quotes by requesting the same coverages and limits from multiple insurers. Provide accurate information, including your Social Security number and vehicle identification number (VIN). Quotes may differ from actual premiums due to inaccuracies or changes in your record.

Ask about discounts and how changing deductibles affects premiums.

Questions to ask agents:
- Are they licensed?
- What coverage limits are available?
- What does state law require?
- Should I buy comprehensive and collision coverage? What deductibles are available?
- What isn’t covered?
- What are the uninsured/underinsured motorist deductibles?
- How do credit and driving records affect premiums?
- How do accidents (fault or not) impact my insurance?
- Can I use my own repair shop?
- Are my family members or other drivers covered?

### Your Responsibilities
Your auto insurance policy is a legal contract. Keep it in your home, understand its terms, and contact your insurer with questions. If you don’t receive your policy within 30 days, follow up. Keep proof of insurance in your vehicle, and maintain records of all related documents and communications. Regular vehicle maintenance is your responsibility to keep it in good condition.

## If You’re Involved in an Accident

Exchange information with the other driver: name, address, insurance company, and policy number. If unavailable, record driver’s license and license plate numbers. Share your info as well.

- Call 911 if someone is injured.
- Move drivable vehicles out of traffic.
- Collect details:
  - Other vehicle’s make, model, year, license plate.
  - Witness contact info.
  - Police officer’s name, badge number, contact info, and accident report details.
  - Date, time, location, weather, road conditions, and take photos or sketches.
- Report hit-and-run or stolen vehicle to police promptly.

## Filing a Claim

Read your policy to understand coverage and claim procedures. Filing claims affects your premium and renewal chances. If damages are minor, consider paying out-of-pocket.

Contact your insurer immediately using the number on your proof-of-insurance card. Ask about required forms. Cooperate with claims adjusters, who may be employees or contractors. Keep notes and records of all interactions.

If disagreements arise, try resolving with your insurer or involve your agent. You may also have repairs inspected by your own mechanic or seek assistance from your state insurance department. In some cases, hiring a licensed public adjuster or attorney may be appropriate.

## Losing Your Insurance

### Cancellation
Occurs when either you or the insurer end coverage before the policy expires. You can cancel for any reason, but insurers typically require a reason within a limited period (e.g., 60 days). If canceled, the insurer must give advance notice (varies by state). Refunds may be issued.

### Non-Renewal
The insurer chooses not to renew after the policy expires. The insurer must notify you before expiration (usually 30 days). You can also choose not to renew. If you disagree with the insurer’s decision, contact your state insurance department.

### What to Do If You Can’t Find Insurance
If shopping around fails, contact your state insurance department for high-risk driver programs.
