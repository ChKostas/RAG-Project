## Everyone needs health insurance

You never know when you will get sick or injured. Medical bills can add up quickly—into the thousands of dollars before you know it.

There are many different kinds of health insurance. This booklet is written to help you understand the basic concepts of how to use and keep your health insurance. Whenever you come across something you don’t understand, look in your insurance Member Handbook for answers. If you can’t find the answer or don’t understand, call your insurance company and ask for help.

Using your health insurance the right way is a smart thing to do. You can stay healthy, save money, and catch health problems early before they become worse. Work with your doctor. Your doctor will set up a plan for you, tell you what tests and shots you need, and help you stay healthy.

# 1. What You Pay and What You Get

## 1. What You Pay and What You Get

Most health insurance works by sharing the cost of care between the health plan and you (the insured). Sometimes the health plan pays most, if not all, of the cost. Other times, you pay a certain amount first before the insurance pays.

There are three things you may need to pay for:

- **Premiums:** The monthly cost of your insurance, like a phone or electric bill. You pay this to keep your insurance active.
- **Deductible:** An amount you may have to pay each year before your insurance starts helping. Some plans don’t have a deductible.
- **Co-pay and co-insurance:** Your share of the costs for your care. You pay these directly, and your insurance pays the rest.

## 1. What You Pay and What You Get (Detailed)

### 1. Your Premium (Your Monthly Cost)

Your premium is what you pay each month to keep your insurance.  
- You pay your first month’s premium to start coverage.  
- Then, you pay monthly to keep it active.  
- It’s like your phone or electric bill—if you don’t pay, service stops.  
- Not paying on time can cause you to lose your insurance.

In return, you get benefits—coverage for healthcare services.

### 2. Deductible

A deductible is an amount you may have to pay on your own each year before your insurance helps.  
- It applies to services like lab work, x-rays, prescriptions, or crutches.  
- It may have separate amounts for medical care and drugs.

**What counts toward your deductible?**  
- Money spent on doctor visits, lab tests, prescriptions, and medical equipment.  
- Both medical and drug expenses may have separate deductibles.

**Example:**  
If your deductible is $500, your insurance may not pay for most services until you’ve paid $500 out-of-pocket.

**What does NOT count toward your deductible?**  
- Your monthly premium.

*Write your deductible here:* ____________________

**Important:**  
Choosing a higher deductible can lower your monthly premium but means paying more out-of-pocket before insurance pays. Consider your health needs carefully.

### 3. Co-payments (Co-pays)

A co-pay is a fixed amount you pay each time you use your insurance.  
- For example, $20 for a doctor visit, $10 for a prescription.  
- You pay this amount directly.  
- If you miss an appointment without canceling, you may still owe the co-pay.

**Sample insurance info:**

- PCP Co-pay: $15  
- Prescription Co-pay: $15 (generic), $25 (name brand)  
- Emergency Room Co-pay: $100

### 4. Co-insurance

Co-insurance is a percentage of the cost you pay for services or drugs, not a fixed amount.  
- You may be billed for it after a visit or upfront for prescriptions.  
- Usually, you pay either a co-pay or co-insurance, not both.  
- Co-insurance often applies to hospital stays, while co-pays are for doctor visits.

**Example:**  
Sample plan with a $500 deductible and 20% co-insurance:  
- You pay the deductible first.  
- Then, for services, you pay 20% of the cost.  
- The insurer pays the remaining 80%.

*Total healthcare services cost: $3,000*  
- You pay your deductible + 20% co-insurance.  
- The insurer pays the rest.

**Note:** Out-of-network providers (not in your plan’s network) may cost more.  
- HMO/EPO plans usually only cover in-network providers.  
- PPO plans cover most providers but at higher costs outside the network.

*Find your annual out-of-pocket maximum in your Benefits Summary.*  
*Write it here:* ____________________

**Good news!**  
There’s a limit to how much you pay each year, called the **Annual Out-of-Pocket Maximum**.  
- It includes your deductible, co-pays, and co-insurance.  
- Once reached, the insurance pays 100% of covered costs for the rest of the year.  
- This does not include premiums.  
- Higher premiums often mean lower out-of-pocket limits.

**Additional costs:**  
- Higher costs if you see providers outside your network.  
- Costs for services not covered by your plan (e.g., cosmetic surgery).

### The 10 Essential Services (required by law):

Your plan must cover and help pay for these:

- Ambulatory patient services (doctor visits)  
- Prescription drugs  
- Emergency services (ER, ambulance)  
- Rehabilitative and habilitative services and devices  
- Hospitalization (overnight stays)  
- Laboratory services (blood tests, scans)  
- Maternity and newborn care  
- Preventive and wellness services, chronic disease management (e.g., immunizations, screenings, managing diabetes)  
- Mental health and substance use disorder services  
- Pediatric services, including oral and vision care

# 2. How to Use Your Health Insurance

## Don’t wait until you’re sick! Be prepared.

Here are steps to make the most of your insurance:

### STEP 1: Pick a primary care provider (PCP).  
This is your main doctor for regular care. In emergencies, go to the ER or call 9-1-1.  
- If you’ve never had insurance, don’t wait for problems to worsen.  
- Call your doctor’s office to schedule your first visit.

### STEP 2: Make your first appointment.  
- During this visit, get health screenings appropriate for you.  
- Early detection can prevent serious problems.

### Allyson’s question:  
*What should I do now that I have insurance?*  
- Check the box if you:  
  ❏ Have chosen a PCP  
  ❏ Have made or plan to make your first appointment

### STEP 3: Learn about your coverage.  
- Keep your Member Handbook accessible.  
- Write down questions and ask your insurance or healthcare provider.  
- Use the customer service helpline if needed.  
- Keep your Member Handbook in a safe place and know how to contact your insurer online.

### STEP 4: Know where to go when sick.  
- For emergencies, go to the ER or call 9-1-1.  
- For non-emergencies, see your PCP to save money.  
- Know the locations and contact info for your PCP, urgent care, and nearest ER.  
- Keep this info handy.

### STEP 5: Prepare for your office visit.  
- Bring your insurance card, ID, and payment method for co-pay.  
- Know your co-pay amount.  
- Prepare questions for your doctor.

### STEP 6: Choose the right provider.  
- Trust your PCP.  
- If not comfortable, change providers via your Member Handbook.

### STEP 7: Follow your treatment plan.  
- Complete tests, see specialists, and take medicines as prescribed.  
- Don’t stop medicines without your doctor’s approval.

### Allyson’s question:  
*What if I need prescriptions or follow-up visits?*  
- Check the box if you:  
  ❏ Fill prescriptions and follow instructions  
  ❏ Schedule follow-up appointments  
  ❏ Get lab tests or screenings

## How to Keep Your Health Insurance

- Pay your premiums on time to avoid cancellation.  
- Understand your bills and Explanation of Benefits (EOB).  
- If you lose coverage, you may need to wait for Open Enrollment or face penalties.

### Tips to remember:  
- Pay online, by mail, or set up automatic payments.  
- Mark due dates on your calendar.  
- Contact your insurer if you have trouble paying.

Lupe’s question:  
*What if I can’t pay my premium or bills on time?*  
- Always pay on time to prevent cancellation.  
- If you lose coverage, you may have to wait until the next Open Enrollment and could face tax penalties.

### How to Keep Your Insurance

- Understand your bills.  
- Report any life changes to your insurance company and marketplace, including:  
  ❏ Address, phone, email changes  
  ❏ Marriages, divorces  
  ❏ Births, adoptions  
  ❏ Loss of dependents  
  ❏ Disability or income changes  
  ❏ Gaining or losing other coverage (Medicare, Medicaid, employment)  
  ❏ Tax status changes  
  ❏ Citizenship or immigration status  
  ❏ Prison releases or entries  
  ❏ Corrections to personal info

**Important:**  
Failing to report income increases may require repayment of financial assistance.

### Allyson’s question:  
*Why do they need so much information?*  
- It helps determine your eligibility for financial help based on household income.  
- Report changes promptly to keep your coverage and assistance accurate.

### Renewing your coverage:  
- Look for notices in fall about renewal.  
- Some plans automatically renew if no changes are reported.  
- Report any life changes early.  
- If you want to change plans, you can select another marketplace plan, a different insurer, or buy outside the marketplace (but lose marketplace benefits).

**Note:**  
Some plans may not be offered every year. Check if your current plan will be available next year.
