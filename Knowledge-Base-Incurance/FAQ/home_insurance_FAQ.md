# Common Questions

# About Home Insurance

## Buildings Insurance

### Should I insure my building’s structure for the market value or rebuilding cost?

You should always insure your home for the cost of rebuilding it. The rebuilding cost will be different from the market value. The rebuilding cost does not include the value of the site the property is built on, as this is not insured. However, it must allow for rebuilding your home in its current form, including the cost of clearing the site and professional fees. The rebuilding cost could be higher than the market value (for example, if the property is built from stone no longer available locally or includes period or special architectural features). Conversely, it could also be lower than the market value.

Some policies now offer cover based on an overall limit for the rebuilding cost (for example, for a property with no more than five bedrooms, a maximum rebuild cost of £400,000 or £500,000). If you are confident this limit is sufficient to rebuild your home and cover site clearance and professional fees, there is no need to calculate a more precise rebuilding cost. However, such policies may not be suitable for flats or non-standard homes (e.g., listed buildings or properties built from unusual materials).

### How can I work out the rebuilding cost of my home?

You can ask a qualified surveyor to determine the rebuilding cost. There is also a calculator on our website (http://abi.bcis.co.uk) that may help you decide how much to insure your home for. This calculator may not be suitable if your home has special design features or is non-standard. Remember to regularly check that your sum insured is sufficient, as rebuilding costs tend to increase over time.

### Do I need to tell my insurer if I have major building work done, such as an extension?

Yes. It is important to update your cover to reflect any increase in rebuilding cost before work begins. You should also inform your insurer of any work that could increase the risk of damage to your home or contents—such as building an extension, significant refurbishment, or if your home will be unoccupied for an extended period. 

In some cases, you may need to take out extra insurance jointly in your name and that of your contractor to cover the building work. Routine interior work like decorating or replacing fitted units (e.g., new bathroom or kitchen) does not usually require informing your insurer unless it affects rebuilding cost; in that case, you should provide the updated figure.

### How do insurers define ‘storm’ and how do they decide if a storm has caused damage?

A storm is not just bad weather; it involves violent weather conditions such as rain, hail, wind, snow, lightning, or a combination thereof. It can last for a short or long period and affect a large or small area. In all cases, it refers to a period of violent weather likely to cause damage. Bad weather unlikely to cause damage to a well-maintained property is not classified as a storm.

Insurers consider factors such as wind speed at the time and location when assessing whether damage was caused by a storm.

### Will insurance cover damage caused by wear and tear?

Home insurance policies do not cover wear and tear. They are intended to cover damage caused by sudden and unexpected events, not deterioration, breakdown, or failure due to lack of maintenance. Proper home and possessions maintenance is essential, as insurance is not a substitute for good upkeep.

### If I have flood damage, will insurers repair or replace my home on a ‘like for like’ basis, or will they pay for repairs that reduce future flood risk?

Insurance aims to restore you to your previous position. Your insurer will repair your property using materials similar to those damaged. If you prefer repairs in a specific way, most insurers will try to accommodate this if you pay the difference. They may suggest using materials and methods that help protect against future flooding, provided this does not cost more than standard repairs. However, flood-resistant repairs are often more expensive, and you may need to cover the extra costs yourself.

Adapting your home to resist flooding—such as fitting barriers—may involve additional work and costs not included in standard repairs. It may be possible to carry out such work during repairs if you agree to cover the extra costs. For more information, visit: http://www.abi.org.uk/Publications/ABI_Publications_A_guide_to_resistant_and_resilient_repair_after_a_flood_670.aspx.

### Why does it take so long to repair flooded properties?

Severely flood-damaged properties can take several months to repair. The process includes decontamination, cleaning, drying, repairs, and redecoration, with drying alone taking many weeks. Repairing before the property is fully dry can lead to damp and mould issues. A guide on what to expect from your insurer after flooding is available at: [http://www.abi.org.uk/Publications/ABI_Publications_Responding_to_major_floods_What_to_expect_from_your_home_insurer_92a.aspx](http://www.abi.org.uk/Publications/ABI_Publications_Responding_to_major_floods_What_to_expect_from_your_home_insurer_92a.aspx).

### If I trust my builder, can I insist that my insurer uses them?

Discuss with your insurer. Many are flexible and may allow you to use your own builder if their quote is reasonable. You are responsible for ensuring the quality of work, and any issues from poor workmanship will be between you and the builder.

### I’ve heard of an ‘act of God’ exclusion—what is this?

‘Act of God’ refers to events outside your reasonable control, such as natural disasters. The term is not typically used in insurance policies. Insurers do not exclude damage caused by natural disasters or events outside your control; coverage generally includes fire, flood, and theft.

### Will my premiums be lower if I improve security (e.g., good locks) or install smoke detectors?

Many insurers and comparison sites ask about security measures before quoting. Homes with better security often receive lower premiums, and some insurers may refuse quotes for homes with poor security.

## Contents Insurance

### Is money automatically covered?

Most policies cover a limited amount of money (e.g., up to £500) as part of contents cover, excluding cash belonging to your business. Loss of cash is covered only for risks insured under the policy, such as theft from the home. Standard cover may not include money lost or stolen while you carry it on you unless you pay extra for this extension.

### What if my belongings are lost, stolen, or damaged while I am away from home?

Cover for belongings while away is usually an optional extension, available at an extra cost. It typically covers items up to a limit you choose, without needing to list each item. There is often a limit for individual items.

Valuable items like bikes, laptops, or jewelry may require individual listing and specific conditions (e.g., bikes must be in your possession or securely locked when away).

### I lost valuables on holiday. Should I claim on travel insurance or home insurance?

You can claim under either or both policies, but not for the same item’s value twice. Compare limits, exclusions, and potential impact on your no-claims discount. Inform both insurers of the claim, as they may share the payout.

### How much should I insure my contents for?

Insure for the cost to replace belongings with new items of similar quality today. This ensures claims are not reduced due to wear and tear, and your policy remains valid. Some policies offer a total limit (e.g., £40,000 or £50,000). 

Ensure the insured amount exceeds the replacement cost of all contents. Use the following steps:

1. List everything in each room (including loft, garage, shed), covering:
   - Floor coverings: carpets, rugs
   - Furniture: tables, chairs, sofas, cabinets
   - Soft furnishings: curtains, cushions
   - Electronics: TVs, PCs, DVD players, phones
   - Appliances: cooker, fridge, washing machine, microwave
   - Eating/drinking items: utensils, china, glassware, food, drinks
   - Valuables: jewelry, furs, pictures, clocks, cameras
   - Sports/leisure: equipment, bikes, books, toys
   - Garden/DIY: furniture, tools, paint
   - Linen: towels, bedding
   - Clothes: shoes, coats, bags

2. Find out the replacement cost for each item, using online sources, catalogues, or expert valuations for valuables.

3. Check policy limits and declare items exceeding these limits to ensure full coverage.

4. When purchasing new expensive items, verify your insured amount and request amendments if needed.

Update this list annually before renewal to keep the sum insured accurate.

### If I leave my home unoccupied for more than two weeks, will I still be covered?

Check your policy. Many policies restrict coverage if the home is unoccupied for extended periods (e.g., over 30 or 60 days). You must inform your insurer if the property will be unoccupied for a long time. Cover may be restricted or excluded for theft, malicious damage, or water damage during such periods unless precautions are taken (e.g., asking neighbors to check regularly). Additional premiums or precautions may be required.

### Will valuable items and collections be automatically covered?

It depends. You should inform your insurer about valuable items, as this may affect premiums and policy terms. Failure to declare valuable items could hinder claims. Collections should be specifically named in the policy, possibly at an extra cost.

### Can I insist on a cash settlement?

Most policies allow the insurer to repair, replace, or pay for damaged or lost items. They typically use their network of suppliers to keep costs down. If you prefer a cash settlement—e.g., for sentimental reasons—the insurer may agree, limited to the replacement cost. Some may offer vouchers instead of cash.

### Can my insurer insist I use their chosen supplier for replacements?

Most insurers will replace items as economically as possible, often through their preferred suppliers. If you want a different supplier, they may agree if it does not significantly increase costs.

### I share my home with housemates. Why might some insurers not quote?

Some insurers do not cover shared housing because they do not operate in that market segment. Others specialize in insuring house-shares, so you may need to shop around.

### Do I need to notify the police before claiming for lost valuables?

Yes. Reporting theft or loss to the police promptly is often a policy condition before making a claim.

### What does ‘new for old’ mean?

‘New for old’ means your insurer will replace lost or damaged belongings with new items of similar type and quality or pay the cost to replace them.

## Buildings and Contents Insurance

### Will I be automatically covered for accidental damage?

Check your policy, as coverage varies. Many policies include limited accidental damage (e.g., broken glass, damage to toilets, underground pipes). Additional accidental damage cover is often available as an extension, providing broader protection against accidental events, excluding specific exclusions like wear and tear.

### I pay my premium monthly—do I pay more than if I paid annually?

This depends on the insurer. Monthly payments are often via credit agreements, which may include charges. To compare costs accurately, obtain quotes for both payment options from the same insurer.

### What is the difference between buildings and contents insurance?

Buildings insurance covers damage to the structure (walls, roof, outbuildings) and fixtures/fittings. Contents insurance covers personal belongings inside the home. Typically, buildings cover items left behind if you sell the home; contents cover items you take with you. Some items, like carpets and curtains, are usually under contents, while fitted appliances and laminate flooring are under buildings.

If you rent, you can often extend contents cover to include items normally insured under buildings (e.g., garden sheds, fitted kitchens) via policy extensions.

### My upstairs neighbor’s leak caused damage. Will their insurance cover my flat or will I need to claim on my own?

Your neighbor is liable only if responsible (e.g., caused damage through negligence). Proving liability can be difficult, so you may need to claim on your own insurance. If you can prove negligence, your insurer might recover costs from them.

### What is an excess and how does it work?

An excess is the amount you pay towards a claim. For example, if your claim is £500 and your excess is £50, the insurer pays £450.

### I’ve made a claim. Will my premium increase?

Not necessarily, but making a claim might affect your no-claims discount. Frequent claims could lead to higher premiums or additional conditions.
